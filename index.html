Project Summary

  • We explore node selection in extremely low-resource settings (i.e. low vaccine availability) using heuristic immunisation algorithms.
  • We run a modeling study to show that it is more effective to vaccinate bridge nodes rather than vaccinate high-degree nodes.
  • We compare seven (random, degree, age, bhd (Bridge Hub Detector), wchb (Weighted Community Hub-Bridge), mv (Modular Vitality)) selection methods under resource-constrained conditions.
  • We run SIR and Covasim models and compare over existing population and synthetic contact networks data.

Key Results

Here, we show our results on the Zenodo population dataset. We note that :

  • Bridge-based strategies consistently achieve lower deaths compared to degree. The cb and bhd curves (pink, red) cluster near the lower half of death plots across all configurations.
  • In the front-load scenarios on the Zenodo population data (Figure 1, Figure 2, Figure 3), wchb and cb plateau at approximately 2-3 deaths, while degree reaches 4-5 deaths and age approaches 7-8 deaths‚Äîcomparable to the NoVax baseline.
  • The age strategy tracks similarly with NoVax and random baselines in Cumulative Infections across nearly all figures (e.g., Figure 1 to Figure 8). While age-based targeting may reduce individual mortality risk, it provides minimal population-level transmission control.
  • When initial infections are seeded within the largest community (Figure 3, Figure 4, Figure 7, Figure 8), bridge-based strategies show stronger relative performance.
  • When vaccines are deployed immediately, bridge-based methods show pronounced advantages, comparing Figure 1 (front-load) with Figure 5 (trickle) for 80 initial infections.
